Afghanistan Maternal Healthcare Crisis

Since 2021, healthcare access has deteriorated dramatically for Afghan women and children

Learn More

What the Issue Is

In 2021, the Taliban's return to power triggered a dramatic rollback of healthcare access for all Afghans—and none more so than women and girls. Before the takeover, much of Afghanistan's medical system relied on international donors. But fears that funds might be diverted under the new regime led many donors to withdraw support, leaving the country dependent on dwindling humanitarian aid.

Even prior to 2021, maternal and infant health was one of Afghanistan's greatest public-health challenges. Today it is worse than ever:

  • Infant mortality: 43 newborns die for every 1,000 live births.
  • Maternal mortality: 620 women die for every 100,000 births.
  • Gaps in care (2018 DHS): 35% of women received no prenatal visits, and over 50% had no postpartum care.
  • Front-line reports: Among female healthcare workers surveyed, 40% say maternal/infant services and facility conditions have declined sharply since the Taliban took control.

On top of crumbling clinics and shrinking budgets, women and girls are now barred—or too afraid—to work or train as doctors, nurses, and midwives. Many report being beaten or harassed when they travel for work, since Taliban edicts force them to be escorted by a male guardian. As female health professionals leave the country or are pushed out of their roles, Afghan mothers and babies have fewer—and often no—options for skilled care.

Yet the resilience of Afghan women shines through. Community midwives and aid workers continue to find creative ways to deliver life-saving services. With targeted support, they can restore and even expand access to the care every woman and child deserves.

Cultural Context

To grasp why these health setbacks have hit women and infants so hard, we need to look beyond the statistics and into the fabric of Afghan society:

Gendered Care Norms

  • Cultural traditions make female providers essential for women's healthcare
  • Taliban bans on female medical education have cut off this crucial pipeline

Movement Restrictions

  • Women must travel with male guardians, making clinic access difficult
  • Rural families often can't reach medical facilities during emergencies

Brain Drain & Departure

  • Thousands of healthcare professionals have fled since 2021
  • Those who remain face harassment and violence

Data Gaps & Uncertainty

  • Surveillance systems for maternal/infant mortality dismantled
  • True severity of the healthcare crisis likely underreported

Resilience in the Face of Adversity

Despite these barriers, Afghan women continue to organize and advocate for their health rights—hosting clandestine training sessions, mobilizing community support networks, and pressuring aid agencies to find "women-only" delivery channels. Their unwavering determination is the springboard for rebuilding a truly inclusive health system.
